Operational Details

🏍 Bike Parking Setup

Rope off front rows for "Show Bikes" — visible from where riders eat. Assign 1 host or manager outside to direct regular cars away from the display zone. All handicap spots remain fully accessible at all times.

🧱 Kickstand Pucks

Provide small plastic or wood pucks to prevent kickstands sinking into hot Georgia asphalt. Costs almost nothing but screams "these people know bikers." Can be branded with the Applebee's logo or event name.

🧢 Gear Check

Designate a secure area (host stand shelf, a designated booth, or a rolling rack) for helmets and heavy jackets. Keeps table space clear and stops gear from blocking aisles. Simple sign: "Leave your lid, grab your seat."

🪑 Outdoor Seating

Recommendation — Needs Confirmation

If no existing patio, consider roping off 3-4 parking spots near the bike display for temporary high-top tables or folding benches. Lets guests eat near their rides. Manning to confirm feasibility and any corporate requirements.

💡 Lighting

Ensure adequate lighting for the "Show Bike" area after sunset. Chrome needs light to pop. Portable LED work lights or string lights if the lot is dim. Also a safety requirement for foot traffic around parked bikes.

🏁 The Slow Race

What Is It?

The last rider to cross a 50-foot cone lane without putting a foot down wins. It's a crowd-favorite at bike rallies — low speed, high comedy, zero risk of actual crashes. Think of it as a balance contest, not a race.

Setup

Back row of the lot or a delivery lane. Completely separated from active traffic. Cones spaced 3 feet apart. Defined heats (e.g., 7 PM and 8 PM) to keep it organized.

Safety

"Participate at your own risk" signage recommended. Separated from pedestrian traffic. Manning's comfort level with this activity is addressed in Tab 5.

Donation Mechanics

Option A: "Pass the Boot"

Physical voluntary cash collection by charity reps at a designated table. Simplest execution. Zero corporate friction. The charity handles their own money — Applebee's just provides the stage.

Option B: Percentage Night

Applebee's pledges a flat percentage of sales during the event window. More impactful, but requires corporate buy-in. Manning has flagged that corporate approvals are hard-won — he fought to get half-price apps approved.

Option C: Vendor-Donated Giveaways

Instead of running a raffle, vendors donate items (free detail, free pinstripe, gear, etc.) that are given away to attendees who donate. Avoids raffle legality entirely while creating an incentive loop.

⚠ Why NOT a Raffle?

Georgia heavily regulates raffles — ticket sales for prizes require a Sheriff's permit tied to the 501(c)(3). It's complex, slow, and bureaucratic. Not worth it for a monthly casual event. Steer clear unless Manning specifically wants to pursue the permitting process. The vendor-donated giveaway model achieves a similar feel without the legal overhead.

Noise Ordinances — Monroe, GA

• Monroe restricts electronic amplification audible beyond the property line

• No live outdoor bands without a municipal permit

• Music must stay at "patio background" levels — strictly controlled after 9 PM

• Motorcycle exhaust noise is not regulated the same way as amplified music, but keeping revving contained is good community relations

Research in Progress: Specific ordinance code references for Monroe will be compiled in Phase 2.

Police & Community Relations

Recommend Manning loop in Monroe PD ahead of the first event. Not because there's a problem — because getting ahead of it builds goodwill and shows professionalism.

Option to invite an officer for a "Safety Booth" presence. Bikers + veterans + charity = the police should be allies, not adversaries. This framing makes the event look organized and community-positive from day one.
